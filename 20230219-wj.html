6)why did the authors think METTL3 controls GC reactions?

The process of the GC (germinal center) reaction can be broken down into several stages:

  1. Antigen presentation: The GC reaction is triggered by the presentation of antigens to B cells by dendritic cells. Antigens are typically derived from pathogens, but can also be self-antigens in the case of autoimmune diseases.

  2. B cell activation and proliferation: Upon encountering antigens, B cells become activated and start to proliferate rapidly, forming a primary focus. B cells are also aided by helper T cells, which provide additional signals to drive their proliferation and differentiation.

  3. Formation of the dark zone: Within the primary focus, B cells start to migrate and form two distinct zones - a dark zone and a light zone. In the dark zone, B cells undergo somatic hypermutation, a process that introduces random mutations into the genes encoding the antibody variable regions. B cells also undergo class switching, a process that changes the isotype of the antibodies produced.

  4. Selection in the light zone: In the light zone, B cells interact with specialized stromal cells and receive signals that help to refine their antigen specificity. B cells with higher-affinity receptors are selected for survival and undergo further rounds of proliferation, while those with lower-affinity receptors undergo apoptosis.

  5. Differentiation: B cells that survive the selection process exit the GC and differentiate into either plasma cells, which produce and secrete large amounts of antibody, or memory B cells, which can quickly respond to subsequent infections with the same pathogen.

The GC reaction is a highly regulated process that ensures the production of high-affinity antibodies against a wide range of pathogens. However, dysregulation of the GC reaction can lead to the development of autoimmune diseases or lymphomas, making it an important target for therapeutic interventions(https://chat.openai.com/chat/).

The authors conducted three independent experiments in a mouse model injected with LCMV-Armstrong strain. They observed a reduction in the number of GL-7+Fas+ GC B cells, IgDlowCD138+ PC and PNA+Fas+ GC B cells in the flow cytometry analysis, a decrease in IgG levels by ELISA test, and differences in the GC structure in IF in the absence of METTL3. In another experiment, the authors injected KLH(CFA) into mice and found a reduction in the number of IgDlowCD138+ PC and PNA+Fas+ GC B cells in the flow cytometry analysis in the absence of METTL3. So, the authors believe METTL3 controls GC reactions.
